# Morphological Analysis: Plant Design — Minimum Capital Expenditure

**Date:** 2026-05-02
**Technique:** Morphological Analysis (Zwicky Box)
**Focus:** How low can capex go while maintaining a viable path to the full vision?

**Capex range:** $1M (ultra-lean) to $66M (full vertical integration)

## Combinations Evaluated

### Combination 1 — $1-2M
Toll thermal / Toll mini-mill / Port license / Push asbestos to spoke / Open-air hardstand

**Feasible:** Yes. The lightest possible footprint — a coordination layer that owns almost nothing. Revenue from brokering certified material streams, not from owning equipment.

**Best site:** Charleston — mature port authority relationships, commercial toll processors in Southeast corridor.

### Combination 2 — $3-6M
Shared cooperative / Toll mini-mill / NAVSEA piggyback / Stabilize+landfill / Open-air hardstand

**Feasible:** Yes. Cooperative model requires partners but reduces cash outlay. NAVSEA piggyback makes port cost disappear — real estimate closer to $2-3M.

**Best site:** Pascagoula — HII cooperative potential, existing Navy facility access.

### Combination 3 — $16-29M
Containerized pyrolysis / Certified graded / Full berth+crane / Stabilize+landfill / Brownfield lease

**Feasible:** Fights itself. Containerized pyrolysis is mobile philosophy; full berth is permanent. Fix: swap to port license, drops to $8-15M.

**Best site:** Avondale — archetypal brownfield with rail, hardstand, state incentives.

### Combination 4 — $1-3M
Spoke thermal / Toll mini-mill / Port license / Push asbestos to spoke / Brownfield lease

**Feasible:** Yes. Most elegant configuration. Thermal and asbestos stay at spokes; hub is certified aggregation and forwarding node. Much simpler to permit and operate.

**Best site:** Charleston — spoke facilities at smaller Gulf/Atlantic points funnel pre-processed material here for certification and toll processing.

### Combination 5 — $20-32M
Shared cooperative / On-site furnace / Port license / Push asbestos to spoke / Sprung fabric

**Feasible:** Marginal. On-site furnace dominates budget, requires sustained throughput — cold start problem. Fix: defer furnace to year 3-5 based on actual volume.

**Best site:** Pascagoula.

### Combination 6 — $13-24M
Toll thermal / Certified graded / Full berth+crane / Stabilize+landfill / Brownfield lease

**Feasible:** Yes but not distinctive. Most compliance-robust — every stream has a defined pathway. Passes DOD auditor's checklist fastest.

**Best site:** Charleston — defense procurement relationships, certified landfill capacity.

### Combination 7 — $5-10M
Toll thermal / Sort/shear/bale / NAVSEA piggyback / Toll vitrification / Sprung fabric

**Feasible:** Yes. Most complete sub-$10M configuration. Every stream handled, no deferrals. Fastest time-to-revenue — sort/shear/bale produces commodity scrap any mini-mill accepts. Sprung fabric deployable in 60-90 days.

**Best site:** Pascagoula — NAVSEA piggyback credible at Navy-adjacent port, HII as immediate steel customer.

### Combination 8 — $5-9M
Spoke thermal / Certified graded / NAVSEA piggyback / Toll vitrification / Open-air hardstand

**Feasible:** Yes. Highest revenue quality — certified graded commands 15-30% premium over commodity scrap, enables DOD supply chain participation. If spoke thermal is reliable, this is a money machine at low capex.

**Best site:** Pascagoula.

## Most Promising

### 1. Combination 7 ($5-10M) — Fastest to Revenue
Complete, internally consistent, every stream handled. NAVSEA piggyback is the risk; if it holds, this is the configuration. Sort/shear/bale produces saleable output in weeks, not months.

### 2. Combination 4 ($1-3M) — Lowest Viable Capex
Elegant because it properly allocates processing to spokes rather than concentrating at hub. First revenue possible within 90 days of brownfield lease execution. Hub becomes pure value-capture node.

### 3. Combination 8 ($5-9M) — Best Revenue Quality
Certified graded output commands premium pricing and enables DOD supply chain participation. Worth the additional cost if certification contracts are pre-signed.

---

## Unexpected Dimension Interactions

### NAVSEA Piggyback Cascades Through Everything
When logistics drops to near-zero, mid-tier configurations become top-tier. The SHIPS Act / defense industrial base framing makes this not just cost but legitimacy — a facility processing retired Navy vessels with NAVSEA logistics support is nearly immune to NIMBY opposition and has a clear federal funding pathway.

### Spoke Thermal + Push Asbestos Is a System Architecture Decision
Combinations 4 and 8 reveal that if spokes are architected correctly, the hub can be a pure value-capture node. Inverting the assumption that the hub does the hard stuff — processing at spokes, certified aggregation at hub — cuts hub capex by 60-70% and dramatically simplifies permitting.

### Sort/Shear/Bale vs. Certified Graded Is Velocity vs. Margin
Not a capability gap. A two-phase approach — launch with commodity scrap, upgrade to certified at month 18 — lets the operation prove itself before seeking next capital tranche.

## The Minimum Viable Plant

**The MVP is Combination 4 with a Combination 7 upgrade path. First-dollar capex: $1.5-2.5M.**

Three non-obvious decisions make this possible:

### Decision 1: The Hub Is a Certified Aggregation Node, Not a Processing Plant
Every hazardous stream stays at spokes or goes to toll processors. The hub receives pre-processed material, certifies it, and sells it. This requires compliance staff, a quality system, and a covered staging area — not a construction project.

### Decision 2: Start With Sort/Shear/Bale, Not Certified Graded
Commodity scrap sells in weeks. Certified graded requires months of customer qualification. Revenue momentum matters more than margin optimization in month one. The shear-baler is a $400-600K piece of equipment. You can lease it. First revenue in 60 days.

### Decision 3: Port Authority License + NAVSEA Relationship = Full Logistics Stack
Don't own a crane. Don't own a berth. The port authority license gives legal access; the NAVSEA relationship gives political cover and potentially free logistics support. Under $1.5M for everything a $10M berth would provide.

### The Build Sequence

| Phase | Timeline | Capex | What You Build |
|-------|----------|-------|----------------|
| **Phase 0: Beachhead** | Months 0-6 | $1.5-2.5M | Brownfield lease, sort/shear/bale (leased), port license, toll contracts signed. First revenue month 3. |
| **Phase 1: Certification** | Months 6-18 | +$3-5M | Add certified grading line, decontamination wash, XRF fleet. Defense contractor qualification begins. Revenue quality jumps 15-30%. |
| **Phase 2: Thermal** | Months 18-36 | +$2-4M | Shared cooperative thermal unit or containerized pyrolysis. Hub begins processing some hazmat directly rather than tolling. Margin capture increases. |
| **Phase 3: Full Capability** | Year 3-5 | +$5-15M | On-site furnace (if throughput justifies), permanent structures replacing temporary, plasma vitrification. The $50M facility — but built on proven revenue, not assumptions. |

**Total phased capex: $12-26M over 5 years, self-funded after Phase 0.**

This is not a full facility. It is a beachhead. It proves the regulatory pathway, demonstrates throughput, builds certified customer relationships, and does so without betting $50M on volume assumptions. Phase 2 follows when year-one revenue validates the model.

### The Political Multiplier

The SHIPS Act framing is not just cover — it is the business model. A facility that can credibly say "we are retiring the old fleet to build the new one" has:
- **A customer** (DOD)
- **A supplier** (MARAD disposal pipeline)
- **A political constituency** (shipbuilding unions, Gulf Coast senators)

That trifecta exists before a single ton is processed. It is worth more than any equipment configuration.
