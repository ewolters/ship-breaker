# Morphological Analysis: Hub Site Selection

## Random Combinations Evaluated

### Combination 1
Gulf Coast / Louisiana / BRAC site / Gulf Coast NAVSEA / Cement kiln / Rural employer

**Feasible:** Yes. The strongest single combination in the set. Louisiana's TSDF corridor gives genuine regulatory precedent for thermal destruction and asbestos vitrification. BRAC site brings infrastructure without greenfield cost. Cement kiln offtake for vitrified residue is a real commercial pathway used at Gulf Coast facilities — eliminates disposal liability. Rural employer premium fits BRAC geography (think Avondale footprint). Weakness: cement kiln contract must precede the permit.

### Combination 2
Inland river / Virginia / BRAC / Hampton Roads / EAF belt / Unionized port city

**Feasible:** Marginal. Virginia on an inland river undercuts Hampton Roads proximity. Barge transport of ship sections adds handling and cost. Fix: swap to Mid-Atlantic Navy homeport (Norfolk/Philadelphia). With that fix, Elizabeth River or Delaware River site would be genuinely strong — BRAC + EAF belt + NAVSEA HQ.

### Combination 3
Gulf Coast / Mississippi-Alabama / BRAC / DC corridor / Mid-Atlantic scrap / Hampton Roads military

**Feasible:** No. Mid-Atlantic scrap offtake makes no sense for Gulf Coast — Southeast EAF cluster is the natural pull. DC corridor is a lobbying play that doesn't need geographic proximity. Fix: swap commodity to Gulf Coast export, federal to Gulf Coast NAVSEA. Interesting: Hampton Roads military labor in Gulf Coast setting is real — veterans relocate for industrial wages.

### Combination 4
Inland river / Louisiana / Greenfield / Pacific NW NSY / Cement kiln / Gulf Coast petrochem

**Feasible:** No. Pacific NW contracts for an inland Louisiana site is dead. Fix: swap to Gulf Coast NAVSEA. Interesting: inland river + cement kiln is a strong sub-combination — barge transport of vitrified residue to Mississippi River cement plants is low-cost logistics.

### Combination 5
Pacific NW / Texas / Greenfield / Pacific NW NSY / Cement kiln / Hampton Roads military

**Feasible:** Partially. Texas jurisdiction has no authority in WA/OR. Fix: swap to Washington (Ecology Dept, Hanford-adjacent hazmat frameworks) and labor to unionized port (Tacoma/Seattle trades). Interesting: Puget Sound NSY + greenfield near Port Angeles or Gray's Harbor could be purpose-built around Bremerton's ship retirement schedule.

### Combination 6
Pacific NW / Texas / Greenfield / Pacific NW NSY / Cement kiln / Rural employer

**Feasible:** Same Texas mismatch. Pacific NW rural employer (Grays Harbor County, post-mill-closure) is plausible — 80 industrial jobs would be transformative. Cement kiln co-location harder in Pacific NW — lacks Gulf Coast/Midwest kiln density. Fix: swap commodity to Pacific NW export (direct to Korea/Japan).

### Combination 7
Gulf Coast / Mississippi-Alabama / Operating scrap yard / Hampton Roads / Cement kiln / Rural employer

**Feasible:** Yes, with correction to Gulf Coast NAVSEA. Underrated. Mississippi/Alabama permitting is genuinely fast. Operating scrap yard means existing metal handling, permitted facility, workforce that understands the commodity. Rural employer fits Moss Point or Chickasaw. Fastest path from site control to first torch.

### Combination 8
Inland river / Louisiana / Operating scrap yard / DC corridor / EAF belt / Hampton Roads military

**Feasible:** Yes with caveats. Louisiana secondary metals operations (near Baton Rouge/New Orleans) + EAF belt offtake via barge to rail is logistically sound. DC corridor lobbying proximity is the most defensible use of that dimension — if MARAD contracts and IRA cost-sharing are the revenue anchor, Hill proximity matters during the authorization window. Most "policy-first" combination in the set.

## Most Promising

### 1. Combination 1 — The Louisiana BRAC Play
Gulf Coast / Louisiana / BRAC / Gulf Coast NAVSEA / Cement kiln / Rural employer

Strongest overall. Regulatory precedent, inherited infrastructure, captive Navy contracts via Pascagoula/HII, closed-loop residue offtake through cement kiln partnership, and a loyal workforce in a low-cost rural labor market. Louisiana's TSDF history means the permitting argument is won before the application is filed.

**Concrete candidate:** Avondale Shipyard footprint (Westwego, LA) — former Northrop Grumman shipbuilding facility, already industrially zoned, deep-water Mississippi River access, within the NAVSEA Gulf Coast contracting orbit, and surrounded by cement and petrochemical infrastructure.

### 2. Combination 7 (corrected) — The Mississippi Fast-Track
Gulf Coast / Mississippi-Alabama / Operating scrap yard / Gulf Coast NAVSEA / Cement kiln / Rural employer

Fastest path from decision to operations. Mississippi/Alabama permits move through thin agency queues. Acquiring an operating scrap yard gives day-one RCRA familiarity, commodity buyer relationships, and metal handling infrastructure. Low capex on site prep. Pascagoula proximity for Navy contracts.

**Concrete candidate:** Moss Point or Chickasaw industrial corridor near Pascagoula — existing scrap operations, deep-water access via Pascagoula River, and proximity to both Huntington Ingalls and Gulf Coast cement kilns.

### 3. Combination 8 — The Policy-First Configuration
Inland river / Louisiana / Operating scrap yard / DC corridor / EAF belt / Hampton Roads military

Best for the contracting and authorization phase (years 0-2). If MARAD captive contracts are the revenue anchor and IRA cost-sharing is the capex enabler, proximity to the Hill during the authorization window is worth geographic inefficiency. Can be executed as a Phase 1 hub with Phase 2 relocation to a port site after contracts are secured.

## Unexpected Dimension Interactions

### Cement Kiln Is Consistently Load-Bearing
Appears in 5/8 combinations. Treated as peripheral by the box structure, but it solves the hardest operational problem: what to do with vitrified asbestos residue that has no clean disposal pathway. Pairing it with Louisiana (existing kiln operators on the Mississippi) or inland river logistics makes it revenue-adjacent offtake, not just waste disposal. **This interaction should probably be a primary dimension in the next iteration.**

### Operating Scrap Yard Outperforms BRAC on Speed
BRAC sites carry environmental liability, federal disposition processes, and community politics. A private scrap yard acquisition can close in 90 days with existing permits, equipment, and commodity relationships. For a first facility trying to prove the concept, the scrap yard path reaches first revenue faster.

### Federal Contract Dimension Splits Into Two Phases
The DC corridor option is not about operations — it's about authorization. The Gulf Coast/Hampton Roads options are about execution. These are different phases of the business with different geographic optima. A two-phase site strategy (DC-adjacent policy office + Gulf Coast operational hub) may outperform any single geographic choice.

## South Carolina Assessment

SC doesn't appear in the random draw, and the combinatorics partially explain why. The dimensions cluster around three strong attractors — Gulf Coast industrial, Hampton Roads federal, Pacific NW Navy — and SC sits between all three without anchoring any.

However, SC is not uncompetitive. A plausible SC configuration:

| Dimension | SC Option |
|-----------|-----------|
| Port | North Charleston / Georgetown (Mid-Atlantic Navy homeport) |
| Jurisdiction | SC DHEC (comparable to VA DEQ, defense-familiar) |
| Site Readiness | Former Charleston Naval Shipyard (BRAC, existing drydock) |
| Federal Contracts | Between Hampton Roads and Gulf Coast orbits; strong Armed Services Committee representation (Lindsey Graham factor) |
| Commodity Offtake | Nucor Berkeley County EAF mill is literally in Charleston metro |
| Labor | Charleston defense industrial base + military transition pipeline |

**The honest assessment:** SC competes on the federal contracting dimension — proximity to Norfolk, existing defense relationships, and congressional leverage. **The Nucor Berkeley County mill is a genuine differentiator** — having your primary steel buyer in the same metro area eliminates commodity transport cost entirely and enables just-in-time alloy-sorted delivery at premium pricing. SC's weakness is hazmat regulatory precedent — DHEC is not Louisiana LDEQ, and there's no established TSDF corridor to point to.

SC would be the strongest choice if the Navy contracting relationship is the primary risk to de-risk first. If hazmat permitting speed is the binding constraint, Louisiana or Mississippi wins.
